Damnatio ad

By John West
& Gabe Isman

1607

George Kendall

George Kendall was executed by firing squad in Jamestown colony in 1608. Kendall's execution marks the beginning of capital punishment in the colonies that would become the United States.

A smithy in Jamestown, James Reed, had struck the president of the colony and was condemned to hang. Before his sentence was carried out, however, Reed implicated Kendall—a member of the Jamestown council—in some kind of conspiracy. Reed was granted a repreive, and a firsthand account of Jamestown reads, “Mr Kendall was executed; being shott to death for a mutiny.”

1622

Daniel Frank

Daniel Frank was executed by hanging in Jamestown colony in 1622. Frank—or “Francke” as he known in a Jamestown record—stole a calf from a weathly landowner, ate it, and hung for his crime.

1633

Jane Champion

Jane Champion was executed by hanging in Virginia in 1632. Her crime is unknown. Champion’s death marks the first execution of a woman in the colonies that would become the United States.

1639

Napaduck

Napaduck, a Pequot man, was executed by hanging in Virginia in 1639. Several hundred Native Americans have been executed under various capital punishment statutes. However, it is difficult to separate extrajudicial and military massacres from the supposedly sanitized killings of men such as Napaduck.

1641

Jan Creoli

Jan Creoli was executed by strangulation then burning in New Netherland (New York) in 1641. His death marks the first execution of an African-American in the colonies that would become the United States. Creoli was executed for sodomizing a 10-year-old child. According to a New Netherland record, he was executed through Biblical precedence: “the prisoner is sentenced to be conveyed to the place of public execution, and there choked to death, and then burnt to ashes”

1642

Thomas Graunger

Thomas Graunger was executed by hanging in Plymouth colony in 1642. His death marks the first execution of a minor in the colonies that would become the United States. He was sixteen or seventeen when found guilty of bestiality. A record of the Plymouth Plantation describes it:

“He was this year detected of buggery, and indicted for the same, with a mare, a cow, two goats, five sheep, two calves and a turkey. Horrible it is to mention, but the truth of the history requires it.”

The record goes on to cite Leviticus and detail the execution of the violated animals.

1643

Achseh Young

Achseh, or Alice, Young was executed by hanging in Hartford, CT in 1643. Her death marks the first execution of a witch in the colonies that would become the United States. Witch-killings were conducted lawfully in the colonies for five decades, culminating in the Salem witch trials of 1692, in which twenty accused witches were hung or pressed.
